ISO/IEC 42001 is the international standard for an **AI management system (AIMS)** — a structured way to govern how your organisation develops, procures and uses AI. Getting "ready" means putting in place the policies, roles, risk processes and evidence that an independent auditor would expect to see. In practice, readiness starts with three things: knowing where AI is actually used in your organisation, deciding who is accountable for it, and agreeing how you assess and treat AI-related risk. Everything else builds on that foundation. You do not need a large team or a finished AI strategy to begin — you need an honest inventory, clear ownership, and a repeatable way to make and record decisions. This guide outlines what the standard covers, who tends to need it, and the first practical steps to take. _[Placeholder content — illustrative, not formal compliance advice.]_

## What is ISO/IEC 42001?

ISO/IEC 42001 defines requirements for establishing, implementing, maintaining and continually improving an AI management system. Like other ISO management-system standards, it is built around leadership commitment, risk-based thinking, defined processes, and evidence of operation. _[placeholder]_

It is **certifiable**, which means an accredited body can audit your AIMS and issue a certificate. That external check is often what gives boards, customers and regulators confidence. _[placeholder]_

## Who needs it?

Organisations that build or rely on AI in ways that carry real consequences — for customers, finances or compliance — are the typical candidates. _[placeholder]_

- Regulated organisations expected to demonstrate responsible AI use
- Providers whose customers ask for assurance over AI
- Teams that want a defensible, repeatable governance baseline

## How to get ready

Readiness is mostly about turning ad-hoc practice into a system. A sensible sequence:

### 1. Build an AI inventory

List where AI is used, by whom, and for what decisions. You cannot govern what you cannot see. _[placeholder]_

### 2. Assign accountability

Name the people accountable for AI governance and define how decisions are escalated. _[placeholder]_

### 3. Establish AI risk assessment

Agree how you identify, assess and treat AI-specific risks, and how you record those judgements. _[placeholder]_

### 4. Close gaps and gather evidence

Map your current state to the standard, fix the gaps, and keep the evidence an auditor will ask for. _[placeholder]_

## Common pitfalls

- Treating it as a documentation exercise instead of a working system
- Starting from tooling rather than from risk and accountability
- Forgetting that the standard expects **continual improvement**, not a one-off project
